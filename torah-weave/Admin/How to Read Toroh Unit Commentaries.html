How to Read Torah Unit Commentaries

A Guide to Understanding the Woven Structure

What is a Unit Commentary?

Each Torah unit commentary page presents the biblical text in its woven structure—a two-dimensional arrangement that reveals meanings invisible in traditional linear reading. This guide explains how to navigate and understand these pages.

Page Organization

Every unit commentary follows the same structure:

  1. The Woven Torah Text (at top) - Primary content with color-coded patterns
  2. Reading Instructions - How to read the specific structure
  3. Commentary Analysis (below text) - Detailed explanations of patterns and meanings

Important: The biblical text always comes first. Readers who just want to see the woven structure can stop after the text section.

Understanding the Woven Text

What is Woven Reading?

The Torah was composed as a literary tapestry. Just as fabric requires both warp threads (vertical) and weft threads (horizontal) to create a complete weave, so Torah units arrange text in two dimensions to create layers of meaning:

  • Linear reading (one-dimensional): Read verse by verse in sequence
  • Woven reading (two-dimensional): Recognize how passages relate through position in the structure

Both are necessary. Linear reading tells you what happens. Woven reading reveals why it's arranged this way and what the arrangement means.

How to Read the Tables

Step-by-Step Reading Method:

  1. Read across each row from left to right before moving to the next row
  2. Notice verse numbers - they show the original biblical sequence
  3. Look for color-coded text - highlights show structural patterns
  4. Pay attention to cell labels - they indicate position in the matrix (e.g., "2aA" = Row 2, thread a, sub-section A)
  5. Read the row header - explains what that level represents

Example: Reading a Simple 2×3 Matrix

Imagine a unit with 2 columns and 3 rows:

Understanding the Color Coding

Color-coded text highlights repeated phrases and patterns that reveal the unit's structure. Each color serves a specific function:

Standard Color System

BLUE (Horizontal Parallels)
Marks parallel phrases or themes that appear across both warp threads (columns). Shows how elements in thread a relate to corresponding elements in thread b.
Example: "divide the light from darkness" appears in both Day 1 and Day 4
GOLD (Vertical Threads)
Marks patterns that run vertically within a single column. Shows thematic continuity or progressive development down one thread.
Example: "Elohim called" appears three times in thread a (naming pattern)
PINK (Closure/Envelope)
Marks phrases that frame the unit—appearing at beginning and end to create an "envelope" structure. Shows boundaries and inclusio patterns.
Example: "heaven and earth" at Genesis 1:1 and 2:1
PURPLE (Chiasm)
Marks chiastic (inverted parallel) structures where elements appear in reverse order. Shows A-B-B'-A' patterns.
Example: Elohim-heaven-earth / earth-deep-Elohim in Genesis 1:1-2
LIME (Internal Parallels)
Marks parallels within a single cell or closely related cells. Shows micro-patterns and detailed correspondences.
Example: Repeated phrases within a single day's creation account
TEAL (Secondary Horizontal)
Additional horizontal parallels when a unit has multiple layers of correspondence.
MAROON (Tertiary Horizontal)
Third level of horizontal parallels for particularly complex units.

Important: Not every color appears in every unit. The color coding reflects the specific structural patterns of each text.

Key Terminology

Structural Terms

Warp Threads (Columns)
The vertical organizing principle. Usually labeled as threads a, b, (c, d, etc.). Warp threads often represent complementary aspects or contrasting perspectives.
Weft Threads (Rows)
The horizontal organizing principle. Usually numbered (Row 1, Row 2, Row 3, etc.). Weft threads often represent sequential development or hierarchical levels.
Matrix
The complete grid structure of a unit. Described by dimensions like "3×2" (3 rows, 2 columns) or "5×2" (5 rows, 2 columns).
Cell
A single position in the matrix where a warp thread intersects a weft thread. Labeled like "2aA" (Row 2, thread a, subsection A).
Sub-rows
Subdivisions within a row, often labeled A, B, C. Can be simple indicators of parallel elements OR genuine sub-rows that must be read horizontally across both columns.
Regular Unit
A unit with systematic, symmetrical structure (e.g., 3×2, 5×2, 4×3). All rows have the same pattern.
Irregular Unit
A unit with asymmetrical structure, often using "framework rows" at beginning and end (e.g., 13331 means: 1 row, 3 rows, 3 rows, 3 rows, 1 row).
Framework Rows
In irregular units, the opening and closing rows that frame the core content. Often establish authority or create closure.

Literary Terms

Chiasm (Chi Structure)
Inverted parallel structure (A-B-B'-A') where elements appear in reverse order. Named after Greek letter χ (chi) which looks like an X.
Envelope (Inclusio)
A framing device where the same word, phrase, or theme appears at beginning and end of a unit, creating boundaries.
Parallel
Corresponding elements that share similar structure, vocabulary, or theme. Can be horizontal (across threads) or vertical (within a thread).
Toledot Formula
"These are the generations of..." phrase that often marks unit boundaries in Genesis.
Death Formula
Standard formula for concluding a character's life ("all the days of X were Y years... and he died"), often marking unit boundaries.

Understanding Divine Names

The Torah uses different names for God, and in woven reading these distinctions are theologically significant:

The Two Primary Names

Elohim (אלהים)

Domain: Natural creation, physical world, cosmic order

Characteristics:

  • Creates through natural law
  • Operates through substance and matter
  • Associated with earth, below, immanent
  • No explicit emotions
  • Requires no offerings

Typical contexts: Creation accounts, natural law, flesh covenants

YHWH (יהוה)

Domain: History, relationship, covenant, supernatural intervention

Characteristics:

  • Acts supernaturally
  • Operates through spirit and heart
  • Associated with heaven, above, transcendent
  • Expresses emotions (sees, regrets, loves)
  • Receives offerings

Typical contexts: Covenant making, personal relationship, judgment, redemption

Divine Name Distribution

In many Genesis units (especially Units 5-16), divine names distribute systematically by rows:

  • Row 1: YHWH acts and speaks (heaven, above, transcendent)
  • Row 2: Both names operate (interface, connection between realms)
  • Row 3: Elohim acts and speaks (earth, below, immanent)

This distribution maps theological geography onto literary structure, showing heaven (YHWH), intermediate space (both), and earth (Elohim).

YHWH Elohim (Compound Name)

In Genesis Unit 2 (Garden of Eden), the compound name "YHWH Elohim" appears consistently, suggesting the two aspects unified in sacred space. This unified name appears nowhere else in Torah with such consistency. Its fracture at Eden's gate (Unit 2) marks cosmic separation.

Reading the Commentary Sections

After the woven Torah text, each unit page includes detailed commentary sections. These are optional—readers interested only in seeing the structured text can stop after the tables. But the commentary reveals why the structure matters.

Standard Commentary Sections

  1. Unit Overview
    • Why this text forms a distinct unit (boundary markers)
    • Position within the book's architecture
    • Linear summary of the narrative
  2. Structural Analysis
    • Matrix dimensions and type (regular/irregular)
    • Warp threads explained (what each column represents)
    • Weft threads explained (what each row represents)
    • Visual matrix with all cells analyzed
  3. Detailed Commentary
    • Cell-by-cell analysis
    • Pattern recognition (horizontal, vertical, diagonal)
    • Thematic development through the structure
    • Literary techniques and devices
  4. Theological Implications
    • Divine name analysis and significance
    • Emergent meanings visible only in woven reading
    • Connections to larger Torah themes
  5. Synthesis
    • Unit's contribution to book structure
    • What woven reading reveals that linear reading misses
    • Reading implications
  6. Study Questions
    • Questions for deeper exploration
    • Unresolved issues
    • Connections to consider
  7. Cross-References
    • Related units within the same book
    • Parallel units in other books
    • Intertextual echoes

Tips for First-Time Readers

Start Simple

  1. Begin with Genesis Unit 1 - The creation account is the clearest example of woven structure and serves as paradigm for reading all units
  2. Read linearly first - Understand the narrative in normal sequence
  3. Then read the woven structure - See how the arrangement reveals additional meaning
  4. Notice one color at a time - Start with blue (horizontal parallels), then explore others
  5. Ask "why is this here?" - For each element, consider why it occupies that specific position

What to Look For

  • Repetition: Words or phrases that appear multiple times, especially in parallel positions
  • Contrast: Elements that differ between threads (columns) or levels (rows)
  • Progression: Development or transformation from top to bottom or left to right
  • Symmetry: Balanced structures, chiasms, envelopes
  • Position: Where something appears in the matrix often signals its significance

Common Questions

Q: Do I have to read the commentary to understand the text?
No! The woven text stands alone. The commentary explains patterns you might miss, but many readers enjoy discovering patterns themselves first, then reading the commentary to see what they found and what they missed.
Q: Why are verse numbers out of order?
Verse numbers show the original biblical sequence. In woven reading, we rearrange verses according to their structural relationships, not their linear order. The numbers help you see where each passage appears in the traditional text.
Q: What if I don't see the patterns?
That's normal! Woven reading is a learned skill. Start with the color-coded highlights—they point you to the patterns. With practice, you'll begin seeing patterns even in uncolored text.
Q: Is this the "correct" way to read Torah?
Woven reading is a complement to, not replacement for, traditional linear reading. Both are necessary. Linear reading tells the story; woven reading reveals the architecture. Think of it like reading music: you need both the melody (linear) and the harmony (structural) to understand the composition fully.
Q: Who decided these structures?
The structures are discovered, not imposed. Objective markers (toledot formulas, death formulas, paragraph breaks, divine name patterns, vocabulary parallels, chiasms) indicate how M (the Torah's composer) organized the text. The commentaries document what's observable in the text itself.
