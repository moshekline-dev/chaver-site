Woven Texts:
Literary Form in Ancient Hebrew Legal Codes

 

Introduction: A Mystery Tradition

Today, familiarity with the principles of hypertext allows us to look at the written word from a perspective not easily available a generation ago. Until very recently, a reader was exposed only to linear writing. Now that we are accustomed to “jump around” in hypertext, we can more easily conceive of yet other possible literary forms. In this article I want to introduce an ancient, and heretofore unrecognized, form: "woven text". The oldest known example of this type of text, the decalog, (2nd millenium BCE), was considered so remarkable in its own era, that its origin was considered supernatural. This original woven text was reputedly written by the “hand of God” on tablets of His creation. The writing on the tablets must itself have been one of the secret mysteries of the ancient Hebrew religion, for the tablets were hidden within the ark of the covenant and never shown in public.

Literary analysis of the decalog, as well as other legal codes in the Torah, has led me to identify the format I refer to as “woven texts” or “literary tapestries”. The discovery of the literary form of these texts makes it possible to see levels of meaning that are otherwise invisible. All of the texts under discussion appear to be almost haphazard collections of laws. Reading them according to their formal literary structure reveals them in an entirely new light. I have chosen the "weave" analogy because the “fabric” of these texts is best understood as having two distinct but interlocking components. The full significance of the analogy will become clear after we examine sample texts and clarify the workings of a loom. Here is a brief schematic description of the decalog as a woven text.

In the above figure A and B represent the two tablets of the decalog. Each has five laws written on it. The laws are paired. Each numbered pair has a common subject, five subjects (1-5) all told. Each tablet represents a coherent "thread" or line of thought. In this way each of the ten units is defined as the intersection of two lines of thought, one from the common subject of the row and one from the constant aspect of the column. For now we can think of this format as a simple table, no different say, than a railroad time table. In fact, when first confronted with the phenomenon, I thought that I was seeing a literary version of a simple table. Later I will explain why the analogy of the weave is more appropriate. So far, this phenomenon has been identified in two distinct literary works, the Torah (Pentateuch) and the Mishnah, the third century compendium of Jewish law. In both cases the format is used for organizing legal codes. Five different codes using the “woven text” technique have been identified in the Torah. The Mishnah contains over five hundred chapters, each of which is a separate weave. The formatted Mishnah chapters can be seen in Hebrew in my book Hamishnah C’Darcah.

The two sources of woven text appeared in periods separated by as much as a thousand years, or more, depending on the exact dating of the Torah. Nevertheless, the structure of Mishnaic chapters demonstrates that its author was intimately familiar with the workings of the literary loom. Where did his knowledge come from? Was it part of an uninterrupted “mystery” tradition extending from the author of the Torah to Rabbi Judah the Prince, the author of the Mishnah? In order to begin to explore this and other intriguing questions, we must first clarify some of the principles of  the ancient textual weave.

The Literary Loom

For those acquainted with the different types of looms, the one to be described here as the “literary loom” should be pictured as a simple tapestry loom. In its most basic form, this consists of a wooden frame with rows of pegs or nails across the top and bottom of the frame. The threads of the warp are stretched between the pegs on the top and bottom of the frame. The weft, or woof, is then woven across the warp. The two components have directional sense, the warp is vertical while the woof is horizontal. There is also a constant difference between the threads. The warp consists of thin colorless threads. Their function is to hold the weave without being seen. The picture or pattern in the tapestry consists solely of the woof.

How is the text woven?[1] Each thread of the woof consists of a set of textual elements, usually a pair or a triad, such as the five pairs in the above illustration. They are identified as a set by standard literary techniques. Usually they have a common subject. Often, they have within them recurring words or phrases. The common subjects of the woof sets create the tapestry, paint the “picture”, of the text. Where are the hidden threads of the warp? They are found in a fixed relationship between the elements of the woof. In the example in The Art of Writing the Oral Tradition the elements of each pair are ordered so that the first reflects the attitude of “love” towards the common subject of the pair (the thread of woof) while the second element expresses fear or awe. In this way we find that the warp consists of the “threads” of love and fear. The fixed distinction between the tablets of the decalog is "singular and plural"; the first tablet indicating the individual or private aspect of the common subject while the second tablet carries the plural or public aspect. In all cases there is a regular difference between the warp and the woof: the warp points to a constant relationship between the vertical components, while the woof presents substantive similarities within the threads of the horizontal line.

In order to see these texts as weaves, they should be organized as tables with the woof appearing in rows and the warp in columns. This is the rule I followed in Hamisnah C’Darcah. It follows from the pattern of the decalog. The ten Words were divided between two tablets. In terms of the formal presentation of the text, the stone tablets are similar to the columns of the warp. Each tablet is one thread of the warp. The five pairs of Words are written across the two tablets like the woof woven across the warp. This is the literal meaning of “The tablets were written across from each other; (first) on one and (then) on the other were they written.” (Exodus 32.15)

According to the Biblical narrative, the tablets predate the written text of the Torah. We might infer from this that woven texts appeared on tablets, or even as tabular text on parchment, before the linear text appeared on parchment. This would explain certain inconsistencies in the Torah. Here is a woven text, like the decalog. A and B represent threads of the warp, while five threads of weft are marked 1-5. Thus, ten "bits" of text are marked from 1A to 5B. How do we write the two dimensional composition as a linear text and still preserve as much of the character of the weave as possible? There are two possibilities. We can copy the linear text row by row: 1A, 1B, 2A, 2B.... . Alternatively, we can copy it by columns: 1A, 2A, 3A, 4A, 5A, 1B, 2B... . The Torah scroll uses both types of translations in different places, the horizontal, by the woof, and the vertical by the warp. This is the inconsistency I referred to. Our texts of the Mishnah, on the other hand, uniformly use horizontal translation. The significant fact in both cases is that the woven text was lost. Rabbi Akiva (2nd century) seems to have been aware of the origins of the legal codes in the Torah when he states in the Mechiltah that we must view them as tables in order to understand them fully. We can only speculate as to why the texts were originally written as weaves, and why succeeding generations lost the ability to understand them as such. In order to even begin speculating, we must observe a few more of the characteristics of these fascinating texts.

One of the reasons I have chosen to present the texts as “tapestries” is that they exhibit a peculiar form of logic, one that is appropriate only for visualized text. I must make the distinction here between “aural logic” and “visual logic”. The triads within these texts consist of two elements in apposition and a third intermediate element. In Hegelian terms, we can think of them as thesis, antithesis and synthesis. And that is exactly “the rub”. “Aural logic” requires that we be presented with the pair in apposition before the intermediate element. Only in that way are we prepared to grasp the third element as a synthesis. “Visual logic”, on the other hand, is positional, the middle is in the middle: thesis, synthesis, antithesis. All of the texts under discussion exhibit visual logic. This fact would seem to indicate that they were originally conceived as written documents. It may also shed some light on the development of the texts that have reached us.

If they were created as woven texts, they could not simply be read in public. The reader, much as the scribe presented earlier, would have to decide whether to read across the rows or down the columns. This could create confusion for the general population who did not have access to the non-linear written document. One time they might hear it in the order of the rows, and another time in the direction of the columns. In order to avoid this problem, a standardized “reader’s edition” of the Torah was prepared, perhaps at the time of Ezra the Scribe. This is our Torah scroll. It is the “oral” or recited form of the Torah, as opposed to the original written Torah that included woven texts. This theory is sufficient to explain the appearances, at least the appearances presented up to now. Unfortunately, I must add some details of the woven texts which may change the picture.

I have already noted the secrecy surrounding the tablets of the decalog. Their concealment stands in bold contrast to the original form in which the content of the Words was transmitted, directly from God to each person present at Sinai. The oral component of the text was heard directly by all the listeners- as stated in Exodus- according to the woof. They knew the words of the Words. Nevertheless, Moses was compelled to hide the text written on the tablets. Why? What did the tablets contain that was not transmitted to all by the mouth of God? A text written across two tablets --- this was the secret. Whatever additional information was encoded in the Words as a textual weave, was to remain a secret between Moses and God. Moreover, the tablets were concealed in the ark of the covenant in the holy of holies, the focal point of the ancient Hebrew religion. A mystery text hidden at the very heart of Judaism. There are indications that the esoteric, or "hidden" aspect of the text. Pointed to the principles of what became known as Kabbalah. This theory will be presented in a separate article.
