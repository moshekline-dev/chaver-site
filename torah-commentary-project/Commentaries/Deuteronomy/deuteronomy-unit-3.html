Deuteronomy Unit 3: The Decalogue

 

Structure: 4×2 with Chiastic Framework | Verses: Deuteronomy 5:1-6:3 | Theme: The Second Decalogue in Context

 

A New Speech Begins: With the words "Hear, O Israel" (שמע ישראל), Moses begins a new speech that will extend through multiple units. This unit opens the second major section of Deuteronomy and forms part of a larger pattern: Units 1-6 are organized in three pairs, with this unit vertically aligned with Units 1 and 5 in the book's structure. While Unit 2 concluded Moses' first speech by placing YHWH at the center of the law, Unit 3 now presents the Decalogue itself—but with significant variations from the Exodus version that reflect the mediated nature of this second giving.

Understanding the Second Decalogue

With the iconic call "Hear, O Israel" (שמע ישראל), Moses begins a new speech that marks a major transition in Deuteronomy. This is not merely a continuation of his previous discourse but the opening of a new section that will extend through multiple units. The placement of this unit is significant within Deuteronomy's larger architecture—it aligns vertically with Units 1 and 5, suggesting a structural relationship that spans the book.

The Structural Context

Deuteronomy's first six units are organized in three pairs:

  • Units 1-2: Moses' first speech (historical revision → legal framework)
  • Units 3-4: The "Hear, Israel" speech begins (Decalogue → consequences)
  • Units 5-6: Continuation of covenant teaching

Unit 3's vertical alignment with Units 1 and 5 creates an additional organizing principle—a column that may represent key transitional moments in Moses' rhetoric.

The Key Discovery

Within this larger framework, Unit 3 presents one of the most sophisticated examples of biblical weaving. The Decalogue (Ten Commandments) is embedded within a carefully constructed chiastic framework that explains why this version differs from the one in Exodus:

  • Column a (Direct): All commandments identical to Exodus
  • Column b (Indirect): All commandments contain variations

This pattern is revealed and reinforced by the framework Moses constructs around the Decalogue, creating an outstanding example of the art of the Biblical weaver.

What makes this unit particularly remarkable is that Moses (or the master weaver "M") has created an entirely new five-pair structure from the same Ten Words. By strategically modifying five of the commandments while leaving five unchanged, M demonstrates that the divine paradigm of woven text can be transformed while maintaining its essential organizing principles. This transformation from the Exodus Decalogue's five consecutive pairs to Deuteronomy's five non-consecutive pairs provides incontrovertible evidence that the original Decalogue was meant to be read as a woven structure.

The variations in the Decalogue continue Moses' revisionist approach established in Unit 1. There, Moses systematically revised wilderness narratives—taking credit for Jethro's judicial innovation, shifting blame for the spies from YHWH to the people, and creating new theological frameworks for the conquest. Here in Unit 3, Moses applies the same revisionist technique to divine law itself, transforming the cosmic Decalogue into a document suited to his immediate rhetorical purpose: preparing the second generation for successful conquest of the land.

The Chiastic Framework

The framework rows (1 and 4) create a chiastic structure that illuminates the Decalogue rows (2-3):

The Chiastic Connections

  • Row 1 → Row 3: The mode of receiving (direct/indirect speech) connects to the mode of human action (direct/indirect harm)
  • Row 4 → Row 2: The response to revelation (direct encounter/mediated encounter) connects to the divine basis of law (immediate/mediated relationship)

The Direct/Indirect Pattern

The column consistency reveals the theological principle behind the variations:

Column a: Direct Connection

  • All commandments exactly as in Exodus
  • Represents unmediated divine-human relationship
  • Focus on immediate actions and consequences
  • The "pure" law as originally given

Column b: Indirect Connection

  • All commandments contain variations
  • Represents mediated relationship (through Moses)
  • Additions emphasize Moses' role: "as YHWH commanded thee"
  • The law as interpreted and applied

Analyzing the Variations

Every variation in Column b follows the indirect/mediated pattern:

Sabbath Command

  • Exodus: "Remember" (זכור) - immediate mental act
  • Deuteronomy: "Keep" (שמור) - ongoing observance
  • Addition: "as YHWH thy God commanded thee" - emphasizes mediation
  • New rationale: Links to slavery in Egypt, not just creation

This shift in the Sabbath rationale continues Moses' pattern of revisionist history from Unit 1. Just as Moses there reframed the spies narrative to shift blame from YHWH to the people, and claimed credit for Jethro's judicial system, here he transforms the cosmic creation rationale into a historical liberation narrative. The Sabbath is no longer primarily about imitating divine rest after creation but about remembering human slavery and divine redemption—a distinctly Mosaic reinterpretation that serves his rhetorical purpose of preparing the people for conquest.

Honor Parents

  • Addition: "that it may be well with thee" - practical benefit
  • Addition: "which YHWH thy God giveth thee" - territorial context

False Witness

  • Exodus: "false" (שקר) witness
  • Deuteronomy: "vain" (שוא) witness - broader prohibition

Coveting

  • Structure: Split into two separate commandments
  • Order: Wife listed separately and first
  • Verb change: "desire" (תתאוה) for property vs. "covet" (תחמד)

The Five-Pair Transformation: From Consecutive to Non-Consecutive Pairs

The variations between the Exodus and Deuteronomy versions of the Decalogue are not random but follow a sophisticated pattern that creates an entirely new five-pair structure. By changing exactly five Words (3, 4, 8-10) while keeping five unchanged (1, 2, 5-7), M creates a new arrangement where:

  • Tablet 1 (Unchanged/Primary): Contains Words 1, 2, 5, 6, 7
  • Tablet 2 (Changed/Derivative): Contains Words 3, 4, 8, 9, 10

The genius of this transformation is that the derivative commandments in Tablet 2 all relate to their primary counterparts in Tablet 1. For example, "do not covet your fellow man's wife" (9) derives from and prevents "adultery" (6). This relationship of primary-to-derivative perfectly mirrors the relationship between the Exodus Decalogue (primary divine revelation) and the Deuteronomy decalogue (Moses' derivative recitation).

The Hierarchical Progression

Both arrangements maintain the same hierarchical flow:

  • Pair A: YHWH in history - the foundation of all that follows
  • Pair B: YHWH and persons - divine-human relationships
  • Pair C: Persons - human relationships at the basic level
  • Pair D: Their wives - family relationships
  • Pair E: Their property - material possessions

This transformation demonstrates that M was not merely copying the Decalogue but creatively reinterpreting it according to the same divine paradigm. The woven structure remains intact even as the specific arrangement changes.

The Art of Biblical Weaving

This unit demonstrates sophisticated literary techniques:

Structural Brilliance

  • The framework doesn't just surround the Decalogue—it interprets it
  • Column consistency reveals the organizing principle
  • Chiastic structure reinforces the direct/indirect theme
  • Interwoven connections create multiple layers of meaning
  • The five-pair transformation proves the original was meant to be read as pairs

Theological Implications

The structure suggests that:

  • The Exodus Decalogue represents the ideal, direct divine-human encounter
  • The Deuteronomy version acknowledges the reality of mediated relationship
  • Both versions are necessary—the pure and the applied
  • Moses' mediation doesn't diminish but contextualizes divine law
  • The transformation itself becomes a teaching about how divine law adapts to human circumstances

This pattern of revision serves Moses' larger rhetorical strategy across Deuteronomy. As the Unit 1 commentary observes, Moses systematically omitted Sinai and the Tabernacle from his historical review, grounding his authority instead in his role as interpreter of Israel's experiences. Now in Unit 3, when he finally addresses the Decalogue, he doesn't simply repeat it—he transforms it, demonstrating his authority to reinterpret even divine law for the new generation's circumstances. The indirect column (b) with all its variations represents Moses doing what he has been doing throughout Deuteronomy: taking divine material and making it speak to the immediate needs of conquest and settlement.

Literary Mastery

M's achievement here is remarkable:

  • Creates an entirely new five-pair structure while using the same Ten Words
  • Maintains all the organizing principles of the original
  • Makes the changes meaningful—each variation serves the primary/derivative theme
  • Provides "incontrovertible testimony" that the Exodus Decalogue was woven

Conclusion: Understanding Through Structure

Deuteronomy Unit 3 marks a pivotal moment in the book's structure. The "Hear, O Israel" that opens this unit signals not just a new speech but a new mode of address—moving from historical retrospective to immediate covenant renewal. The structural patterns—both the internal chiastic framework and the larger alignment with Units 1 and 5—work together to emphasize this moment of divine-human encounter, even as the variations in Column b acknowledge the necessary mediation of Moses.

This unit stands as "an outstanding example of the art of the Biblical weaver," demonstrating how structure itself can serve as commentary, revealing theological truths through literary form. The beginning of the "Hear, Israel" speech with the Decalogue establishes the pattern that will continue through the paired units that follow.

Most significantly, this unit provides M's own incontrovertible testimony that the Exodus Decalogue was created to be read as five consecutive pairs. Without first understanding the paired structure in Exodus, we could never recognize the sophisticated transformation in Deuteronomy. The fact that M could create an entirely new five-pair structure using the same Words—maintaining both the hierarchical themes (YHWH → persons → wives → property) and the primary/derivative relationship—demonstrates mastery of the woven text paradigm that goes beyond mere imitation to creative transformation.

The transformation reveals that woven text is not a rigid format but a living paradigm capable of adaptation while maintaining its essential principles. Just as the tablets were given twice—first directly, then through mediation—so too the literary structure demonstrates both continuity and transformation, showing how divine patterns can be reinterpreted for new circumstances while maintaining their fundamental truth.

Moses' treatment of the Decalogue epitomizes his role throughout Deuteronomy as both faithful transmitter and creative interpreter of divine law. The same Moses who in Unit 1 boldly revised historical narratives to serve his rhetorical purposes now demonstrates that even the most sacred text—the divine Words themselves—can be transformed when filtered through human mediation. Yet this is not corruption but completion: the Deuteronomy Decalogue shows how divine law lives and breathes through human interpretation, adapting to new circumstances while maintaining its essential structure and truth.
